1. Research Question

Among Medicare patients hospitalized for acute stroke who were discharged home and received their first SLP contact in an outpatient/clinic setting, does earlier initiation of that clinic SLP associate with lower rates of aspiration pneumonia, dysphagia diagnoses, and G-tube placement at 1 year post-discharge?

Exposure: Timing of first outpatient (clinic-based) SLP contact after discharge, categorized as:

GroupDays from dischargeRole
0-14d0 to 14 daysTreated (Comparison A)
15-30d15 to 30 daysTreated (Comparison B)
31-90d31 to 90 daysReference (both comparisons)

Two pairwise PSM comparisons:

Comparison A 0-14d vs 31-90d

Very early SLP (within 2 weeks of discharge) vs. late outpatient SLP (weeks 4-13). Captures effects of near-immediate post-acute SLP referral.

Comparison B 15-30d vs 31-90d

Standard-early SLP (weeks 2-4) vs. late outpatient SLP. Captures the effect of timely standard-of-care referral.

3.2 Inclusion Criteria

1 Inpatient admission with acute stroke as principal diagnosis
ICD-10-CM codes: I60 (SAH), I61 (ICH), I63 (Ischemic), I64 (Unspecified)
Admitted via MEDPAR inpatient claim; principal diagnosis only (not secondary)
2 First (index) stroke admission
Earliest qualifying inpatient admission per beneficiary; no prior stroke claim (I60/I61/I63/I64) in the 6 months before admission
3 Continuous FFS Medicare enrollment
Must have both Part A and Part B coverage for 6 months prior to and at least 1 day after discharge (to capture post-discharge services)
4 Age 65+
Standard Medicare FFS beneficiary age cutoff

3.3 Exclusion Criteria

X Hospice discharge
Discharge status codes indicating hospice at index hospitalization — patients unhealthy at baseline may confound results
X Death within 30 days of discharge
Patients who die before the earliest possible SLP contact cannot be classified into any timing group
X Stroke sequelae (I69) or TIA (G45) as principal diagnosis
These are not acute index strokes
5 Home discharge
Index admission discharge status code 01 (routine home), 06 (home with organized home health), or 07 (AMA). Patients discharged to SNF, IRF, LTACH, or other post-acute settings are excluded. Rationale: SLP timing is clearly defined and similarly accessible across this population; non-home settings introduce facility-level confounding in SLP access.
6 First SLP contact must be clinic-based (outpatient or carrier)
Patients whose first SLP contact within 90 days of discharge was through HHA (home health SLP) are excluded. This ensures both comparison groups are ambulatory enough to access outpatient clinic-based care, eliminating functional status confounding driven by HHA use asymmetry. Applied via first_slp_is_clinic = TRUE in stroke_slp.

4. SLP Exposure Definition

4.1 What Counts as SLP for Timing

SLP timing is based on clinic-based SLP only: carrier claims (HCPCS codes) and outpatient facility revenue centers. HHA and SNF SLP are excluded from the timing definition. This ensures the timing exposure reflects ambulatory, voluntary outpatient engagement rather than facility-provided care driven by discharge setting.

HHA and SNF revenue center 044x claims are not counted toward SLP timing. Patients whose first SLP (of any type) was HHA-based are excluded from the analytic cohort entirely (see inclusion criterion 6).

4.2 Timing Calculation

days_to_slp_outpt = date of first qualifying clinic SLP claim − index discharge date.
If no qualifying clinic claim exists within 90 days: days_to_slp_outpt = NULL (excluded from analytic cohort).

-- Computed in stroke_slp.sql (_slp_outpt_only = carrier + outpatient only)
days_to_slp_outpt = MIN(claim_date) - index_dschg_date
                    WHERE claim_date BETWEEN index_dschg_date
                                        AND index_dschg_date + INTERVAL 90 DAY
                      AND (hcpcs IN (slp_eval, slp_tx, slp_swallow)
                           OR revenue_center BETWEEN '0440' AND '0449')
                      AND claim_type IN ('carrier', 'outpatient')

4.3 Timing Group Assignment

Assigned in stroke_propensity.sql. All patients in the analytic cohort have a clinic SLP within 90 days by definition; three timing levels are used.

CASE
    WHEN days_to_slp_outpt BETWEEN  0 AND 14 THEN '0-14d'
    WHEN days_to_slp_outpt BETWEEN 15 AND 30 THEN '15-30d'
    WHEN days_to_slp_outpt BETWEEN 31 AND 90 THEN '31-90d'
END AS slp_timing_group

Discharge disposition (Home vs. Home+HHA) is included as a covariate in the PS model. Because all patients are home-discharged, it is not a strong confounder, but captures the marginal difference between planned-HHA and non-HHA home discharges. It is no longer used as an exact matching stratum.

5.2 PSM Algorithm

  • Logistic regression: P(treated=1 | covariates), fit separately for each comparison
  • Logit-transformed propensity score used for distance metric
  • 1:1 greedy nearest-neighbor matching without replacement
  • Caliper: 0.2 × SD(logit PS) (Austin 2011 recommendation)
  • Single KD-tree across all home-discharged controls (no exact matching stratum required)
  • Random shuffle of treated patients before matching to remove order effects (seed = 42)
  • Results written back to stroke_propensity: psm_matched_A, psm_match_id_A, prop_score_A (and _B for comparison B)

6. Outcomes

All outcomes are measured in the 365-day window following index discharge date. Time-to-event is measured from index discharge date for descriptive analyses; for the primary time-varying Cox, person-time begins at discharge and the exposure switches at days_to_slp_outpt.

6.1 Primary Outcomes

Outcomes directly targeted by SLP swallowing rehabilitation — most likely to be modified by timing of SLP initiation.

Aspiration Pneumonia Primary

Any inpatient or outpatient claim with a principal or secondary aspiration PNA diagnosis within 365 days of discharge. SNF and HHA claims are excluded from ascertainment.

J690, J698

Source: Inpatient, carrier, outpatient facility only

8. Statistical Analysis

8.1 Primary Analysis

For each PSM comparison (A and B):

  1. Time-varying Cox proportional hazards — PRIMARY Addresses immortal time
    Person-time is split at days_to_slp_outpt for each patient:
    • Segment 1: [0, days_to_slp_outpt) — trt=0 for all patients (neither group has yet received SLP)
    • Segment 2: [days_to_slp_outpt, censor] — trt=1 for treated group, trt=0 for reference group
    The HR on trt estimates the hazard of the early-SLP group vs. late-SLP group after SLP initiation, with pre-SLP immortal time correctly attributed as unexposed person-time for both groups.
    For non-mortality outcomes (aspiration, dysphagia), death is treated as a competing event (censored).
  2. Odds ratios with 95% CI (Woolf method) at 90d / 180d / 365d — descriptive; measured from discharge date
  3. Kaplan-Meier survival / cumulative-incidence curves with log-rank test — descriptive; measured from discharge date
  4. Mann-Whitney U test for 1-year Medicare cost distributions
OR tables and KM curves are measured from discharge date (day 0) and are therefore subject to immortal time bias, particularly at the 90-day timepoint. They are reported for clinical interpretability alongside the primary time-varying Cox results.

8.2 Subgroup Analyses

  • By stroke type: Ischemic vs. ICH
  • By age: <75, 75–84, 85+
  • By dysphagia POA: present vs. absent
  • By Elixhauser tertile: Low / Mid / High comorbidity burden
  • By discharge disposition: Home (codes 01/07) vs. Home+HHA (code 06) — both are within the analytic cohort; results reported separately as the planned-HHA subgroup is clinically distinct.

8.3 Covariate Balance Assessment

Standardized mean differences (SMD) reported pre- and post-match for all PSM covariates. Balance threshold: SMD < 0.1 considered well-balanced.

10. Key Design Decisions

Why Early vs. Late (not SLP vs. No SLP)?
A naive SLP vs. No SLP comparison is heavily confounded by indication: sicker, more dysphagic patients are more likely to receive SLP and have worse outcomes. Even after PSM, residual confounding is expected because there are unmeasured severity variables. Restricting to patients who did receive outpatient SLP (varying only in timing) dramatically reduces this confounding and addresses a more actionable clinical question: does it matter how quickly we refer?
Why restrict to home-discharged patients with clinic-first SLP?
In the full cohort, home-discharged patients who received SLP at 0–14 days had dramatically better outcomes than those at 31–90 days — but HHA use was severely asymmetric (14.6% vs. 47.5%). Patients in the late-SLP group were largely homebound and receiving SLP through HHA, while early-SLP patients were ambulatory clinic-goers. This is functional status confounding, not a treatment timing effect. By requiring that the first SLP contact be clinic-based (not HHA), both groups are drawn from the same functionally ambulatory population, making the timing comparison valid.
Why 1-year follow-up?
Median time to aspiration pneumonia event is ~289 days post-discharge; 56% of 5-year events occur within year 1. SLP's mechanism (swallowing rehabilitation) operates primarily in the first 6–12 months of recovery. Competing risks from death and enrollment disenrollment severely dilute signal in years 3–5. Primary window: 365 days; secondary analysis can extend to 2 years if warranted.
Why no landmark analysis?
Landmark analysis (conditioning survival at day 30) was considered but rejected: (1) only 0.8% of the cohort had aspiration PNA before day 30, so the landmark provides negligible survival selection benefit; (2) the cohort already excludes 30-day post-discharge deaths, eliminating the primary motivation for a landmark; (3) the PSM on timing groups is the methodologically preferred approach for a timing-based exposure.
